> - **Irradiance vs. Irradiation:** Irradiance is the "instant" power (kW) hitting your roof; Irradiation is the "total" energy (kWh) collected over the course of the day.

## ‚òÄÔ∏è The Physics: Defining the Solar Resource
The sun hits the upper atmosphere of Earth with a "Solar Constant" of approximately **1,361 Watts per square meter (W/m¬≤)**. However, as that light travels toward your roof, it must pass through the atmosphere, where it is scattered, absorbed, and reflected.

### The "1-Sun" Benchmark
In the solar laboratory, panels are tested under a standardized condition called **1-Sun**, which is exactly 1,000W/m¬≤ at 25¬∞C. In the real world:
- At 9:00 AM, the sun is at a low angle. The irradiance might only be 200W/m¬≤.
- At 1:00 PM, with the sun directly overhead, the irradiance might hit 1,050W/m¬≤.
- At 4:00 PM, it drops back down to 300W/m¬≤.

**The Math:** Instead of tracking these fluctuating numbers, we "compress" all the solar energy received into a single metric. If your roof receives 5,000 watt-hours (5kWh) of total energy throughout the day, we say you have **5 Peak Sun Hours.**

## üß™ Atmospheric Filtration: The Invisible Thieves
Two homes at the exact same latitude can have different Peak Sun Hour profiles based on local climate.
1. **Humidity & Water Vapor:** High humidity absorbs infrared radiation. This is why a "Dry" sun in Nevada is more productive than a "Humid" sun in Florida, even if both are cloud-free.
2. **Elevation (Air Mass):** The higher you are, the less atmosphere (Air Mass) the light has to penetrate. A home in Denver (5,280 ft) receives a "sharper" sun that generates more energy than a home at sea level.
3. **Aerosols & Pollution:** Smoke from wildfires or urban smog scatters photons, turning "Direct" light into "Diffuse" light. While panels can still make power from diffuse light, the [Peak Production](/articles/maximizing-solar-yield) drops significantly.
