> - **The 10% / $1,000 Rule:** Most courts define an "unreasonable restriction" as anything that increases system cost by more than $1,000 or reduces production by more than 10%.
> - **Aesthetics vs. ROI:** An HOA can request "reasonable" aesthetic modifications (like black frames), but they cannot force you to move panels to a north-facing roof where they won't work.
> - **Historic Exemption:** Generally, homes in federally designated Historic Districts have different rules where preservation laws can sometimes override solar rights.

## ‚öñÔ∏è The Legal Shield: Understanding Solar Access Laws
Currently, more than 30 states have enacted **Solar Access Laws**. These laws vary in strength, but their core purpose is to prevent "private governments" (HOAs) from interfering with a citizen's right to generate their own power.

### The "Unreasonable Restriction" Threshold
The most critical part of these laws is how they define "unreasonable." If an HOA board asks you to move your panels from the front of the house to the back for "aesthetic reasons," you must check that request against two metrics:
1. **Cost Increase:** Does moving the panels require extra wiring or specialized mounts that add more than a $1,000 to the bill?
2. **Production Loss:** Does moving the panels to the shaded north side of the house [decrease your energy production](/articles/impact-of-shade-on-solar) by more than 10%?

If the answer to either is **Yes**, the HOA's request is legally "unreasonable" in states like California, Arizona, and Florida.

## üèó The "Aesthetic" Compromise: What the HOA CAN Regulate
While an HOA usually cannot say "No," they are legally allowed to regulate the **how**. In 2026, "reasonable" restrictions typically include:
- **Low-Profile Mounting:** Requiring panels to be mounted flush (parallel) to the roofline rather than tilted up on racks.
- **Conduit Management:** Requiring all electrical conduit (the silver pipes) to be run through the attic or painted to match the trim of the house.
- **Hardware Consistency:** Requiring "All-Black" modules (black cells, black frames, black backsheets) so the array blends into dark shingle roofs.
- **Visibility:** Consolidating the panels into one uniform rectangle rather than several scattered "islands" across the roof.

### Step 3: The Neighbor Solar Easement
In rare cases where you are worried about a neighbor planting a tree that might shadow your panels in the future, you can negotiate a **Solar Easement**. This is a legal agreement recorded on both property titles. It ensures that your neighbor will never build a structure or plant a tree that blocks the "Sun Space" above your roof.

## üìù When Can an HOA Actually Say "No"?
There are three specific scenarios where an HOA still holds significant power:
1. **Common Areas:** In a condo or townhome development, the roof is often a "Common Element." You cannot install panels on a shared roof without 100% board approval and a structural amendment.
2. **Historic Districts:** If your home is in a federally or state-designated Historic District, the laws governing "Historical Preservation" often supersede Solar Access Laws. You may be forced to use [integrated solar shingles](/) or back-of-roof placement only.
3. **Safety Violations:** If your proposed layout blocks the "Fire Setback" paths (as required by the fire marshal), the HOA (and the city) will rightfully deny the permit.
