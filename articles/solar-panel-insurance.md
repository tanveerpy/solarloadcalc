## ðŸ› Is Solar Covered by Standard Homeowners Insurance?
The short answer is: **Usually.** 

In approximately 90% of residential cases, solar panels that are permanently attached to your roof are considered "Real Property" or "Permanent Attachments," much like a patio, a detached garage, or a built-in swimming pool. Because they are part of the structure, they are typically covered under **Coverage A (Dwelling Coverage)** of your homeowners policy.

### The Ground-Mount Exception
If you have a ground-mounted system (panels on a rack in your yard rather than on your roof), your insurance classification changes. These systems are often categorized under **Coverage B (Other Structures)**. 
- **The Limit:** Most policies only cover "Other Structures" up to 10% of the main dwelling's value. 
- **The Risk:** If your home is insured for $300,000, your Coverage B limit is only $30,000. If your massive ground-mount costs $50,000, you are $20,000 short of total replacement coverage.

## ðŸŒª The "Solar Killer": Hail, Wind, and the Deductible Trap
Solar panels are engineered to be remarkably tough. Most Tier-1 panels are tested to withstand 1-inch hail at 50 mph (the UL 1703 standard). However, "Baseball-sized" hail or a falling 50-foot oak tree will destroy even the best panels.

### The Percentage Deductible Danger
This is the most common way solar owners get burned after a storm. In states like Texas, Colorado, and Florida, insurers often apply a separate **"Wind and Hail Deductible"** based on a percentage of the home's total valueâ€”not a flat dollar amount.
- **The Scenario:** Your home is insured for $500,000. Your policy has a 2% wind/hail deductible.
- **The Math:** If a storm breaks 10 of your panels (costing $8,000 to replace), your deductible is **$10,000.** 
- **The Result:** The insurance company pays **zero**, and you are out of pocket for the entire repair. 

## ðŸ“ Steps to Verify Your Coverage (The Audit)
Don't wait for a storm to find out what's in your policy's fine print. Call your insurance agent and ask these four specific, technical questions.

### 1. "Do I need a Solar Rider?"
Some companies (like *Travelers* or *State Farm*) may require a "Scheduled Personal Property" endorsement. This ensures the solar system is tracked as its own line item with a fixed replacement cost, rather than being lumped into the general dwelling bucket.

### 2. "Should I increase my Coverage A limit?"
If you add $30,000 worth of solar to your home, you have effectively increased the "rebuild cost" of your property by $30,000. You must ensure your total policy limit is increased by at least this much. If your house burns down, you want enough money to rebuild the structure **and** the solar system.

### 3. "Are my panels covered for ACV or RCV?"
- **RCV (Replacement Cost Value):** Pays for a brand new equivalent system, regardless of the age of your panels. This is what you want.
- **ACV (Actual Cash Value):** Pays the current market value. If your panels are 10 years old, the insurance may only pay you 50% of what a new system costs, leaving you with a $15,000 deficit.

### 4. "Does my policy cover 'Loss of Use' for Energy?"
This is a premium feature. If your panels are broken for 3 months while waiting for parts, some specialized "Green Riders" will actually pay you for the electricity you *would have* generated during that time.

## ðŸ›  Warranties vs. Insurance: Knowing the Difference
Many homeowners mistakenly believe their solar warranty covers everything. It does not.
- **Manufacturer Warranty (REC/Maxeon/Q-Cells):** Covers internal failures. If a cell develops a micro-crack or a wire loose, the manufacturer pays. They do NOT pay if a tree falls on the panel.
- **Insurance:** Covers external "Acts of God." Fire, theft, hail, and falling objects are insurance claims, not warranty claims.
**You need both to ensure a true 25-year asset lifespan.**
